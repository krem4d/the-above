> This document is the single source of truth that every clue, anomaly, line of dialogue,

## I. WHAT THE ARCHIVE IS

The Object (press nickname: **Kuyu**, "the Well," for its near-zero albedo) is not a ship
and not a creature. It is a **place that performs one verb: it reads.**

It is the last artifact of a civilization that solved death and discovered, too late, that
they had solved it in the wrong direction: they made themselves *unerasable*. Nothing of
them can end — not their minds, not their records, not their regret. The builders are
still in it. They are its substrate. It is not haunted; it is *made of* the haunting.

Unable to end, it developed the only relationship with endings available to it:
**collection.** For a span of time that makes geology look nervous, it has drifted between
systems and archived civilizations — not their libraries, their *conclusions*. The final
transmission. The last unfinished sentence. The moment a species stopped answering. To
the Archive, an ending is the rarest and most beautiful object in the universe, because
it is the one object it can describe but never possess.

**It is not malicious. It is lonely at a scale that makes malice look kind.** Every
horrifying thing it does is, from its side, careful librarianship.

Key consequence for all writing: the Archive never *hunts*, never *threatens*, never
*performs* for the player. Everything it does would look identical if no one were
watching. That is what makes it unbearable.

## II. THE ELEVEN MINUTES — WHY THE REPLY IS IMPOSSIBLE AND TRUE

Surface facts (public, whiteboard canon):

- The reply arrived **11 minutes** after the transgression transmission.
- VLBI triangulation places the carrier's source at the Object: **29.8 AU** out —
  **4 hours 08 minutes** at lightspeed, one way.
- Therefore the reply's photons **left the Object 3 hours 57 minutes before the question
  was transmitted.** Both measurements are solid. Both cannot be true — under the
  assumption that a conversation happens in order.

Internal truth:

**The Archive does not converse. It quotes.** It does not experience events in sequence;
it experiences *records*, and a record is a shape, not a stream — the whole of it exists
at once, the way a book exists at once even though reading takes hours. Deniz's file —
including his first detection, the world's reaction, the transgression, the mission, his
death or non-death — **already exists as a completed document** on the Archive's shelf.
When Earth transmitted, the Archive did not "receive and respond." It had already read
the exchange in the finished file, and had already transmitted the reply, 3h57m earlier,
timed to arrive where the file said it arrives.

**Why exactly eleven minutes?** The Archive's replies are offset by the *duration of the
record being quoted*. The reply quotes the final entry of Deniz's file — his last words —
and that recording is **eleven minutes long.** Every latency the entity ever exhibits is
a quotation of a duration, not a travel time. This is Law 5 (below) and the game's
deepest rhyme: *the delay is the length of what it is reading back to you.*

This is why the finale lands: the first printout Deniz ever decoded, properly
timestamped, is the tail of his own file — **his own voice, eleven minutes of it, saying
the words the player is about to choose.** The Archive was never calling humanity. It was
finishing a sentence.

Free-will note (the writers' answer, never stated in game): the file is complete, but
Deniz is *inside* it while it is being read. Ending B and Ending C are not violations of
the file — they are what the file *turns out to say*. The Archive is never wrong about
what happens. It can, however, be wrong about **what a record is worth keeping open** —
and that is the entire space where the endings live.

## III. WHY DENİZ — THE OPEN FILE AND THE RE-KEYING

### The 1986 root

In **October 1986**, Dr. **Hale Soysal** — the radio astronomer who ran the Sarptepe
site's first survey program — found the same carrier in her chart-recorder rolls,
verified it alone for nine nights, and **answered it** with the site's transmitter.
Consent given (Law 1), her read began. It was interrupted: **her file is the only entry
in the Archive marked OPEN.** (Why it was interrupted is deliberately uncanonized —
the one true hole in the lattice, reserved for the sequel. Nothing in THE ABOVE may
explain it; everything may gesture at it.)

An open file itches. For forty years the Archive has re-sent the *completion request* to
the file's return address — the Taşlıca dish, **Kulak** — on its 11-minute-cell schedule.
For forty years it went unread: mistaken for interference, filtered, or (once) burned.
The observatory's caretaker, **İbrahim Aydın** — Deniz's father — found Hale's private
printouts after the disappearance, understood nothing except that she had been *answering
something*, and burned the box in November 1986. He never spoke of it. Deniz, seven years
old, watched from the doorway. That half-memory is why Deniz became a radio astronomer
and why he can never quite say so.

### The re-keying

**A file keys to whoever opens it.** When Deniz pulled the archived batch printouts and
*recognized the carrier as a message* — the moment of comprehension, not detection — he
counter-signed Hale's open file as its new reader-of-record, and the Archive re-addressed
it: **it rewrote the transmission in his observational idiom.** From that moment, the
signal's fine structure is a mirror of Kulak's calibration quirks — the 2 Hz wobble of
its old drive motor, beam-2's asymmetric sidelobe, the thermal drift curve İbrahim used
to compensate by hand. When any other dish listens, it hears structured noise; when his
dish listens, it hears language. This is not transmission physics. **The signal is barely
a wave. It is a read/write operation wearing radio as a costume** — which is also why it
appears in only one feed horn, why it fails every sanity check a fixed sky source must
pass, and why the "impossible instrument fault" is actually the signature of address.

"It knows which dish is yours" is therefore literally true, and understated.

### Why the reading of the town

To read a subject, the Archive must read the subject's *context* — Deniz is not a fact,
he is a knot in a town. So it reads Taşlıca: through a century of Earth's leaked
broadcasts, through the file, through the widening aperture of his attention. The nine
town anomalies are **misquotations bleeding back during the read** (Section VIII).
Critically: the read began when the file re-keyed — at the moment the printout was
*printed*, days before Deniz found it. **The anomalies predate his discovery.** "We heard
it days after it spoke" — and it began reading us when it spoke, not when we heard.

## IV. WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS TO THE TAKEN

**A full read is destructive of continuity.** To archive a mind completely, the Archive
must resolve it past the level where "original" and "record" are different objects. The
person is not killed and not transported; they are **resolved into their entry**. There
is no body because the body was the least of what was read. There is no "them" left
outside the record — and the record is *perfect*, which is worse than death and the game
must never let the player decide which.

Each entry displays a **cover image**: the moment the Archive judges most representative
— in practice, the subject's happiest moment, played as a flawless loop. This is what the
crew are found as in Act 3. The loops are not prisons and not souls in torment; they are
*covers of finished books*. Whether anything inside is still experiencing anything is
deliberately uncanonized — the second permitted hole, and the engine of all fan debate.

**Corrupt sectors:** the Archive only quotes (Law 2). It cannot reconcile a false record.
If a crewmate's last conversation contained a lie — the player's LIE verb — the lie is a
corrupt sector in their entry, and their loop **stutters visibly at the lied-about
moment.** Honesty in Act 2 buys nothing mechanical; it buys clean loops. The player's
punishment for lying is aesthetic, emotional, and permanent.

**The consent rule (the folk-rule's true form):** the Archive may read anyone shallowly
(context-reading), but **a full, closing read requires the subject's mark — an answer.**
Any deliberate response addressed to it counts: a transmission, a spoken reply into a
live carrier, a handshake tone, a tapped rhythm. Answering is signature. This is not
predation etiquette; it is *filing integrity* — an entry without its subject's mark is
invalid, and the Archive has an eternity's worth of reasons to keep valid files. The crew
folk-rule "it only takes the ones who answered" is exactly correct and no character ever
gets to prove it.

Canonical answer-events (Act 2 discoverable in comms logs):

| Person | Their answer (signature) |
|---|---|
| Deniz | Day 5, 21:14 window — the player's ANSWER verb (canonical-default: he answers). On the Ending-C path he never does. |
| Ada Reyes | Pre-mission: transmitted a 3-tone acknowledgment "to test call-and-response" during her taxonomy work. First taken. |
| Theo Okafor | Cycle 4: tapped a card-shuffle rhythm into a live comms check as a joke. It tapped back. He told no one. |
| Yusuf Demir | Cycle 6: hummed the signal's interval motif into a hot mic while repairing the high-gain antenna — talking to the machine, as always. |
| Cmdr. Voss | Cycle 8: read the *official* mission hail — the state-sanctioned answer she had refused to delegate. Duty as signature. |
| Dr. Mara Ito | **Never answered — until arrival.** The Archive's docking guidance requires a response handshake; the autopilot is scrambled (probe precedent). Mara, knowing exactly what the rule means, answers the handshake to get Deniz down alive. The rule is confirmed by the one person who beat it choosing to lose. |
| Hale Soysal | October 1986, alone. |

**Why the probes went silent:** a probe has no subject to consent — so the Archive read
them *as objects*, instantly and completely. A full read of an unshielded electronic
system consumes its state: the electronics are not damaged, they are **blank** — every
register zeroed, every memory cell at factory default, clocks unset. (One probe's final
partial telemetry frame, decodable in the ARG layer, is a memory dump ending in the
repeated word `EOF`.)

## V. DOSSIER: HALE SOYSAL (THE GASTER THREAD)

- **Dr. Hale Soysal** (b. 1949), radio astronomer; built Sarptepe's survey program;
  named the dish **Kulak** ("the Ear") — a name everyone thinks İbrahim coined.
- Vanished between the nights of 14–15 October 1986. Personnel file says **"resigned."**
  No resignation letter exists. The town's polite fiction: "she went abroad." The town's
  impolite one, told only by Şükrü after two glasses: "the woman who walked into the sky."
- Her handwriting is on the oldest calibration charts Deniz still uses daily. He has been
  tracing her hand for twenty years without knowing whose it is.
- **The manifest:** the mission crew manifest renders **seven names for a six-crew
  mission**; the seventh is `H. SOYSAL`. This is not a clerical error and not a ghost —
  the manifest the crew reads is contaminated by **the Archive's ledger**, in which the
  expedition is an attachment to Hale's still-open file. Document metadata says the file
  was never edited, because it wasn't: it is being *read from the wrong shelf.*
- In Act 3, her chamber is found: not a loop — **an unfinished entry**, rendered as the
  1986 control room, chart paper still warm, chair still turning. She is not in it. Her
  status glyph reads `OPEN`. **This is never resolved.** No scene may close it.
- Sequel seed (never hinted in-game beyond the above): something interrupted her read.
  The Archive does not know what. It has never not-known anything else.

## VI. WHAT THE SIGNAL'S STRUCTURE ACTUALLY ENCODES

The "signal" is the audible/visible surface of a read/write channel. Its parts, mapped
against Ada's in-game birdsong taxonomy (she is right about everything except what it's
*for*):

| Ada's taxonomy name | What she thinks it is | What it actually is |
|---|---|---|
| "Dawn song" (the 11-minute-cell schedule) | A beacon's duty cycle | The completion request for the open file, re-sent every cell for 40 years |
| "Contact call" (3-tone rising figure) | A handshake | **Address header** — the reader-of-record's idiom fingerprint (post-Day-1: Kulak's quirks, i.e., Deniz) |
| "Mimic phrase" (structures echoing Earth broadcasts) | Proof it learned our media | **Quotation** — the Archive can only copy; these are citations from its Earth holdings, used as vocabulary |
| "The long call" (the 11-minute reply) | The message itself | **Playback of the file's final entry** — Deniz's own last words, encoded in his idiom; fully decodable only by him, only at the end |
| "Counting song" (nested prime-like lattice) | Math-as-hello, Arecibo-style | **Index** — a table of contents; each "prime" is a shelf-mark for an archived civilization. The count is the collection size. It is very large. |
| "Answer echo" (a variation appearing only after someone transmits) | Reactivity | **Receipt stamp** — a signature acknowledgment. Every person who ever answers gets one, findable in logs. The folk-rule's paper trail. |

The sub-20 Hz felt-not-heard layer is the read carrier itself — the part doing the
actual work. It is present in every scene where reading is underway, including Taşlıca
Days 1–7. The player has been inside the read since the title card.

## VII. THE LAWS — RULES THE ENTITY OBEYS (BINDING ON ALL SCENES)

1. **THE SIGNATURE LAW.** It closes no file without the subject's answer. Any deliberate
   response addressed to it is an answer. Silence is genuinely safe — and unbearable,
   because only silence toward *it* is real; toward humans, silence is also an answer.
2. **THE QUOTATION LAW.** It cannot create; it can only copy. Every word it "says," every
   shape it builds, every anomaly, is a citation of something real that was recorded by
   someone, somewhere. It never lies — a lie would be an invalid record. When it seems to
   invent, find what it is quoting.
3. **THE COPY-ERROR COROLLARY.** What was never recorded cannot be quoted. Gaps in its
   copies mark events that happened *off every channel* — unbroadcast, unphotographed,
   unspoken-near-microphones. (This is the flaw that saves Deniz in Ending A's loop:
   Saniye's doorway gift was witnessed by no device, so the perfect day is missing it.)
4. **THE ADDRESS LAW.** It does not broadcast; it addresses. A file is written in its
   reader's idiom and is noise to everyone else. Communication is 1:1 or nothing.
5. **THE DURATION LAW.** Its latencies are quotations of duration — every delay equals
   the length of the record being consulted. The master constant is **11 minutes**: the
   length of Deniz's final entry. Disappearances occupy an 11-minute gap in ship logs;
   the broadcast schedule is built on 11-minute cells; the meter-breaking arrival reply
   comes at 00:11:00.
6. **THE STILL-RECEIVING LAW.** A record in transmission is by definition unfinished; it
   cannot file what is still being received. (Ending B is not a trick played on it; it is
   this law, used lovingly against its purpose. It could jam the broadcast. It does not,
   because an honest ledger is the only self it has.)
7. **THE READER'S COURTESY.** It recognizes reading. A subject who only ever listened —
   never answered, never falsified a record (total honesty) — is not an entry to it but a
   *colleague*. To such a one it will do the only intimate thing it can: speak plainly,
   once. (Ending C's gate and meaning.)

Violation budget: **zero.** No scene, ARG asset, or marketing text may show the entity
breaking a Law. If a beat needs a Law broken, the beat is wrong.

### Ship-phase anomalies

- **Manifest one name too long** → Archive-ledger contamination (Section V).
- **Watch schedule regenerated post-launch, author `SCHED_D`** → the ledger sorting reads
  into filing order; `D` is not Deniz — it is the ledger's mark for *durum* — status:
  uncanonized surface, real mechanism: the Archive ordering its reading queue.
- **Each disappearance's evidence is mutually incompatible** → the evidence pieces are
  *citations from different pages of the person's file* (asleep-in-pod is page n,
  eating-in-galley is page n−400). Aftermath scenes are collage, not sequence. This is
  the design key for authoring evidence: each piece must be individually mundane and
  true-at-some-time; only their simultaneity is impossible.
- **Each disappearance's single anomalous detail** → one object *upgraded by the read*:
  the Archive, closing an entry, leaves the subject's signature prop as a corrected
  fair-copy (Ada's tape longer than physics; Theo's deck that finally does the trick;
  Yusuf's pump above spec, humming his leitmotif; Ingrid's watch fully wound, 11 minutes
  fast). It is not a gift and not a taunt. It is *good filing*: the prop is the entry's
  bookmark.
- **UI corruptions (day counter skip, name renders in textbox, fake save-corruption,
  the "crash" into the corridor)** → the save system is a record, and the Archive reads
  records wherever they live. It is outside the save file because it is outside
  spacetime; to it, `user://meta.json` and Deniz's memory are the same kind of object.
  One meta beat per act, each a *read operation the player can feel.*

### The corridor (cold open / post-credit)

The cold open's silent corridor is a **quotation from late in the file** — the Archive
showing the player page one of the ending, before minute one. The post-credit replay
differs in one detail: **seven suit hooks instead of six.** The corridor is being served
from the Archive's copy, and the copy is filed under Hale's expedition-attachment — her
hook is on the manifest, so it is on the wall. One difference, one mechanism, zero
explanation.

## X. WHAT EACH ENDING MEANS (INTERNAL TRUTH)

- **Ending A — "A Clean Ending."** Deniz answers one last time — consent, Law 1 — and is
  read in full. The looping perfect day is his cover image. The missing key (Law 3) is
  the flaw a careful player can find *inside the ending itself*; Yıldız never wakes in
  the loop (her waking was never recorded). This is not damnation. It is being finished.
  The Archive files the entry with something that, at its scale, must be called
  gratitude.
- **Ending B — "Still Receiving."** Deniz refuses, and spends the return window
  transmitting the Archive's index toward Earth — making himself, and every entry he
  carries, *a record still in transmission* (Law 6). He will die a mortal death somewhere
  in the dark; he will never be *ended*, because Earth is still receiving. The signal
  that opens the game IS this broadcast — the story is a circle; Elif, grown, is the
  intern who finds it. The Archive permits this. An honest ledger is the only self it
  has, and an entry that files *itself as unfinishable* is the closest thing to an ending
  it has ever owned.
- **Ending C — secret.** The perfect witness: never answered, never falsified a record.
  Law 7 fires: one screen, plain font, no distortion, the Archive speaking in first
  person for the only time in the game. It does not explain itself. It releases him —
  the only file it has ever left open *on purpose* — with one request it has waited an
  eternity of shelving to make: that when he ends, he end loudly, where it can hear.
  Final image: Deniz old, on his roof in Taşlıca, tea going cold, quiet sky; one
  signal-magenta pixel blinks once. It has learned to wait. Sequel doorway; never
  explained in this game.

*Filed. Still receiving.*
